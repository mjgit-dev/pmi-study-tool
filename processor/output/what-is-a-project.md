# What is a Project

## Definition of a Project

**Core Definition:** "A project is a temporary endeavor undertaken to create a unique product, service, or result."

This definition comes directly from PMI and is the foundation of everything in project management. Understanding what qualifies as a project — and what does not — is essential before any other concept can be applied.

**Bottom line:** A project is defined by its temporariness and uniqueness, distinguishing it from all other types of work.

---

## Key Characteristics of a Project

Every project shares three defining characteristics:

1. **Temporary** — Every project has a definite beginning and a definite end. The work does not go on indefinitely; it concludes when objectives are met, or when the project is cancelled.

2. **Unique Deliverable** — Every project produces something that has not existed in exactly that form before — whether a product, a service, or a result. No two projects are identical.

3. **Progressive Elaboration** — The project plan becomes more detailed as more information becomes available over time. You do not need to know everything at the start; planning deepens as the project moves forward.

**Important Concept:** Progressive elaboration is a formal PMI concept — it means that early-stage planning is intentionally high-level, and detail is added iteratively as the team learns more about the work ahead.

**Exam Tip:** All three characteristics — temporary, unique, and progressively elaborated — can appear on exam questions. If a scenario describes work that is ongoing and repetitive, it is NOT a project.

**Bottom line:** Every true project must be temporary, produce something unique, and allow for planning that grows in detail over time.

---

## Projects vs. Operations

**Key Distinction:** Projects and operations are fundamentally different types of work.

| Projects | Operations |
|----------|------------|
| Temporary — has a defined end | Ongoing — continues indefinitely |
| Produces a unique product, service, or result | Repetitive — produces the same output repeatedly |
| Ends when objectives are achieved | Sustains the business day-to-day |

Operations keep an organization running — think of a factory producing the same product every day, or a help desk answering calls. Projects, by contrast, are initiated to create something new or bring about a specific change.

**Exam Tip:** The exam may present a scenario and ask whether it describes a project or operations. The keywords to watch for are "ongoing," "repetitive," and "routine" (operations) versus "temporary," "new," and "unique" (project).

**Bottom line:** If the work repeats indefinitely with no defined end, it is an operation — not a project.

## Examples of Projects

The instructor provides concrete examples to illustrate the range and variety of projects in the real world:

1. **Building a bridge** — a physical construction effort with a clear start, end, and unique design.
2. **Developing a software application** — a technology initiative producing a unique deliverable.
3. **Organizing a conference** — an event-based project with a definite end date and a one-time result.

These examples demonstrate that projects exist across every industry and can vary enormously in scale, complexity, and domain — yet all share the same three core characteristics.

**Bottom line:** Projects exist in every field and at every scale, but they are always tied together by being temporary, unique, and progressively elaborated.

## Definition of Project Management

**Core Definition:** "The application of knowledge, skills, tools, and techniques to project activities to meet project requirements."

This is the official PMI definition of **project management**. It breaks down into four components applied to project work:

1. **Knowledge** — Understanding of the discipline, processes, and best practices.
2. **Skills** — The competencies of the project manager and team members.
3. **Tools** — Instruments and software used to plan, track, and control work.
4. **Techniques** — Methods and approaches applied to carry out project activities.

The purpose of applying all four is singular: to meet project requirements. Project management is not an end in itself — it is the means by which a project achieves its intended outcome.

**Exam Tip:** PMI's four-part definition — knowledge, skills, tools, and techniques — is a foundational concept. Expect exam questions that test whether you recognize this definition and understand that its purpose is to meet project requirements.

**Bottom line:** Project management is the structured application of knowledge, skills, tools, and techniques specifically to ensure that a project's requirements are successfully met.
